Overview

The Research Question

Does the way you prompt a model matter as much as the model itself? This study tests that hypothesis — running identical content through different prompting strategies and measuring the qualitative difference in output.

01 · Motivation
Beyond Simple Prompting

Baseline prompts produce vague, narrative-style outputs. Structured prompting techniques — CoT, role assignment, temperature control — dramatically change how models parse and present nuance.

02 · Approach
Cross-Document Testing

Three documents were selected to represent a spectrum of writing styles: objective journalism, emotionally-rich personal narrative, and fragmented multi-voice reviews.

03 · Platform
Hugging Face Spaces

All experiments were run on a hosted LLM via Hugging Face Spaces, enabling reproducible configuration across temperature settings, system roles, and prompting frameworks.

Methodology

Six experiments were conducted — two per document type. Each pair isolates a specific variable: technique, role, or temperature.

Experiments

Results & Analysis

Each pair of experiments reveals how prompting choices shape output quality, structure, and depth.

01–02
Document Type · News Article
BBC Coffee Article: Direct vs. Technical Analyst + CoT
BBC News Article Temperature 0.6 Role: Technical Analyst Chain-of-Thought

The article presents balanced scientific claims about coffee — positive effects (polyphenols, cardiovascular benefits) alongside negative ones (caffeine, anxiety). A direct prompt produces a narrative summary; the structured approach forces granular categorisation.

Strategy 1 — Direct Baseline T = 0.6
Produced a narrative summary without clearly distinguishing between positive, negative and neutral sentiments. Informative, but lacked explicit categorisation — findings presented as flowing text rather than structured segments.
Strategy 2 — Technical Analyst + CoT T = 0.6
The model adopted an evidence-based analytical persona, systematically categorising positive sentiment (pros), negative sentiment (cons), and neutral observations (ongoing research, chemical complexity) as separate, labelled sections.
↗
Key Insight · News Document
Both strategies produced satisfactory outputs, but the combined method delivered far more granular and actionable results. The direct approach generated a narrative without distinguishing sentiment polarity — the technical analyst role + CoT forced the model to categorise and justify each sentiment class separately. Role assignment provides appropriate domain framing; CoT ensures transparent reasoning.
03–04
Document Type · Personal Blog
Medium Blog: Creative Assistant vs. Creative Assistant + CoT
Personal Narrative Temperature 0.6 Role: Creative Assistant CoT Comparison

A blog about daily cappuccino spending — emotionally rich, subjective, layered with guilt, indulgence, and self-reflection. The creative assistant role was chosen to match the narrative register of the document.

Strategy 3 — Creative Assistant T = 0.6
Produced a narrative-style interpretation with longer paragraphs. Captured cultural context, referencing millennial "treat yourself" habits. Emotionally resonant but lacked systematic organisation — insights were embedded in flowing prose.
Strategy 4 — Creative Assistant + CoT T = 0.6
CoT addition classified emotions across 7 distinct dimensions: craving & reward, habit formation, social & cultural influences, guilt & enjoyment, exploration & compromise, self-care & ritual, value & identity. A systematic map of complex emotional layering.
↗
Key Insight · Personal Blog
The creative role alone captured emotional nuance but produced unstructured output. Adding CoT transformed this into a 7-dimension emotion map — revealing how coffee functions as self-care, identity expression, and habit ritual simultaneously. This demonstrates that implicit sentiment (embedded in narrative style rather than explicit evaluative statements) requires both the right role framing AND structured reasoning to unpack fully. The combination outperforms either technique alone.
05–06
Document Type · Multi-Voice Reviews
Vice Coffee Reviews: High Temperature Direct vs. Low Temperature CoT
User Reviews T = 1.0 → 0.3 Temperature as Variable Chain-of-Thought

Mixed, conflicting reviews from multiple users — combining strong praise and sharp criticism. This experiment isolates temperature as the primary variable alongside the CoT technique, revealing how randomness affects coherence in multi-perspective content.

Strategy 5 — Direct, High Temp T = 1.0
Produced a mixed overall sentiment with a generalised summary. At T=1.0, increased randomness resulted in less organised output — the model failed to navigate conflicting opinions clearly, producing an unfocused response without distinguishing between sentiment dimensions.
Strategy 6 — CoT, Low Temp T = 0.3
Systematically broken into 5 elements: overall sentiment assessment → positive enumeration → negative enumeration → neutral identification → final synthesis. Recognised aspect-based sentiments across coffee quality, service quality, and atmosphere.
↗
Key Insight · Reviews Document
Temperature was the decisive variable here. At T=1.0, increased randomness degraded output coherence — the model didn't handle conflicting viewpoints well. Dropping to T=0.3 with CoT produced a structured, aspect-based breakdown that distinguishes coffee quality, service, and atmosphere as separate sentiment dimensions. This is directly applicable to business decision-making: structured review analysis with controlled generation outperforms high-creativity settings for analytical tasks.
Performance

What the Results Showed

Prompting strategy — not the model alone — was the decisive factor in output quality. Combined techniques consistently outperformed single-method approaches across all document types.

Combined Technique
Best result
CoT + Role assignment consistently produced the most structured, transparent, and comprehensive outputs across all three document types. No exceptions.
Direct Baseline
Weak structure
Direct prompts produced narrative summaries without categorising sentiment polarity. Informative but not analytical — missing the segmentation needed for actionable insight.
Temperature Impact
T=0.3 wins
Lower temperature (0.3) gave the most direct and consistent results for complex multi-perspective content. T=1.0 introduced randomness that reduced analytical coherence without improving quality.
Synthesis

Four Key Findings

Distilled from cross-experiment analysis — these are the transferable principles for effective LLM prompt design in sentiment tasks.

01
Chain-of-Thought Outperforms Baseline — Always
CoT produced more structured, transparent, and comprehensive sentiment analyses regardless of content type. Whether analysing scientific journalism or mixed café reviews, the step-by-step structure consistently surfaced granular insight that baseline prompts missed. This aligns with Fei et al. (2023) on CoT improving extraction of implicit sentiment from mixed texts.
02
Role Assignment Shapes Cognitive Framing
The technical analyst role produced evidence-based, category-driven outputs ideal for factual content. The creative assistant role captured emotional and cultural nuance that a technical framing would miss. Role-playing narrows the model's stylistic approach, improving relevance for domain-specific tasks — but the role must match the document type to be effective.
03
Temperature Controls Coherence, Not Creativity
Higher temperature (1.0) introduced randomness that degraded analytical quality without improving handling of diverse opinions. Lower temperature (0.3) produced more direct, consistent outputs — particularly valuable for complex, multi-perspective content. Mid-range (0.6) balanced coherence and expressiveness for narrative documents. Temperature selection should be task-driven, not default.
04
Content Type Must Drive Strategy Selection
No single prompting strategy works universally. Objective journalism benefited most from role + CoT. Personal narratives needed creative framing to capture emotional layering. Multi-voice reviews required low temperature + structured decomposition. Prompting strategies must be matched to the document's register and the analytical goal — not applied as a one-size-fits-all default.
