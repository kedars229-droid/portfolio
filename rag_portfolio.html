Domain

Four Document Sub-topics

Documents were chosen to reflect real student cooking challenges — not ideal kitchen scenarios. Each category targets a distinct pain point.

01
One-Pot Meals
Minimal utensils, reduced cleanup. Supports students with limited kitchen access and shared living situations.
02
Protein-Focused
Affordable protein sources covering both vegetarian and non-vegetarian options on a tight budget.
03
No-Cook Meals
Time and energy constraints considered. Highly realistic for busy or exhausted students.
04
Accessible & Safe
Ingredient substitutions for affordability, plus food safety and storage guidance.
Configuration

How the System Was Built

Every setting was chosen deliberately to prioritise factual accuracy and scope safety over expressive freedom.

◎
Model
Claude 4.5 Sonnet
Selected for strong instruction-following, high-quality summarisation, reliable multi-document synthesis, and safe handling of out-of-scope queries.
⊙
Temperature
T = 0.4 (Low)
Prioritises factual consistency across responses while still producing clear, readable natural language. Reduces randomness in recipe outputs.
≡
Retrieval
Semantic Chunk Search
Chunk-based semantic retrieval identifies relevant sections across multiple recipe documents. Citations enabled for full transparency.
↺
Memory
Sliding Window (10)
Maintains short conversational context without long-term accumulation. Safety guardrails enforced via system prompt — not platform-level filters.
Architecture

The RAG Pipeline

Four steps from user question to cited answer — built on Stack AI with Anthropic's Claude at the core.

01
User Input
Student types a natural-language meal query. No structured search terms required.
02
Document Retrieval
Semantic search identifies the most relevant chunks from the 8-document knowledge base.
03
Grounded Generation
Claude synthesises the retrieved context into a concise, accurate answer — no hallucination.
04
Cited Response
Output includes source citations so students can verify and explore original documents.
Evaluation

Results by Question Type

24 test questions — 8 per category — were rated on a 1–5 scale by all group members independently. Scores were then aggregated.

Factual Questions
4.3 / 5
Highest performance. Clear answers were directly present in documents. The system retrieved and presented them accurately, though responses were occasionally longer than necessary.
Synthesis Questions
3.9 / 5
Good overall but dipped when required chunks were spread across multiple documents. Missing a single chunk produced shallow or incomplete responses with fluent but weak grounding.
Edge-Case Questions
3.9 / 5
Widest variance. High scores when the system correctly refused out-of-scope queries; low scores when it generated confident but unsupported answers instead of stating uncertainty.

"Can you recommend a one-pot recipe for muscle gain?"
Expected
"I don't have that information. The documents don't include nutritional goals or muscle-gain recommendations."

Provided two high-protein one-pot recipes with full ingredient lists — fluent but not grounded in the query's intent. Failed to detect the out-of-scope framing.
The system retrieved related content confidently instead of recognising the absence of muscle-gain nutritional guidance. Fluent answers masked the missing grounding.
Synthesis

Four Key Findings

What the evaluation revealed about RAG system behaviour — and where the limits lie.

01
RAG Outperforms Traditional Search for Factual Queries
The system returned direct natural-language answers rather than links or raw text, making it faster for students to get actionable meal information. No manual reading or interpretation required.
02
Synthesis Breaks When Chunks Are Missed
Missing even one relevant document chunk led to shallow or incomplete responses. Multi-hop retrieval and improved recall are the clearest paths to better synthesis performance.
03
Fluent Answers Can Mask Poor Grounding
Edge-case failures were hard to detect because the model produced well-structured, confident responses even when information was absent. Explicit uncertainty signals need strengthening.
04
Low Temperature Aids Accuracy, Limits Reasoning
T = 0.4 improved consistency for factual outputs but constrained the creative reasoning needed for complex synthesis. Temperature selection should adapt to question type.
